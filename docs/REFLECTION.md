# Reflection Answer

## What should be public, what should stay hidden, and what should be decided by AI versus by a human in a bounty system?

In a fair bounty system, **what is public and what is hidden must be carefully designed to prevent information asymmetry**. The bounty rules, judging criteria, reward amount, and deadlines should always be public—participants need full transparency about what they're competing for and how they'll be evaluated. However, **submission content must remain hidden until all submissions are collected**, because exposing answers early creates a "last mover advantage" where later participants can copy or improve upon earlier ideas. This is why the commit-reveal pattern is essential: only a cryptographic hash is visible during the submission phase, preserving fairness.

**The answer content should stay hidden until the judging phase is complete.** In an ideal system, not even the AI judge should see answers before all submissions are locked in. This prevents any form of bias or information leakage. After judging, all answers should be revealed simultaneously so participants can learn from each other and verify the fairness of the process. The commitment hash acts as a binding promise—you can't change your answer after the deadline, and no one can see what you submitted until it's too late to copy.

**AI is best suited for the initial evaluation and ranking of submissions.** AI can objectively compare answers against a rubric, score them consistently, and provide detailed reasoning for each score. This is especially powerful when all submissions are judged together in a single batch, ensuring consistent evaluation standards. AI excels at pattern matching, factual verification, and structured comparison—tasks that would be tedious and potentially biased if done by humans alone.

However, **the final decision on who receives the reward should remain with a human.** AI can recommend a winner based on scores and reasoning, but the bounty owner should have the final say. This is because AI may miss nuances—creative solutions that don't fit the rubric perfectly, ethical considerations, or the strategic value of certain approaches. The human-in-the-loop pattern ensures accountability: if the AI makes an error or the criteria need adjustment, the human can override. This also prevents a scenario where a bug in the AI's reasoning automatically transfers funds without review.

In summary: **rules and criteria are public, submissions are hidden until judging is complete, AI handles the heavy lifting of evaluation, and humans make the final call.** This creates a system that is fair, efficient, and accountable—one where participants compete on the merit of their ideas rather than their ability to see and copy others' work.
